## Reflection

At the beginning of this project, I had several ideas for what dataset I wanted to work with. My initial options included Google Photos, Letterboxd, email history, and YouTube data. I experimented with each of them briefly, became frustrated at different stages, and even tried some of the suggested project ideas before eventually returning to YouTube history analysis.

I first attempted to work with Google Photos because I expected location and timestamp metadata to produce interesting visualizations. However, after exploring the export, I realized that the available metadata was not as useful as I had anticipated. Many of my photos were screenshots rather than original camera images, which meant they lacked geographic data. In addition, each photo came with a separate JSON file, which made the dataset difficult to manage and less practical for the type of analysis I wanted to perform. This experience helped me understand that choosing the right dataset is an important part of data analysis, not just the analysis itself.

Before starting the YouTube analysis, I requested my watch and search history through Google Takeout and waited about an hour for the export to be generated. The export contained only two JSON files, which was significantly easier to work with compared to the Google Photos structure. The YouTube JSON format was relatively consistent and contained the fields needed for analysis, allowing me to focus on transforming the data into a structured format rather than dealing with heavy inconsistencies.

One limitation I noticed was that my exported watch history appeared incomplete. After manually comparing it with my YouTube account history, I observed that activity extended beyond the exported range, which stopped around 2023. I assume this may be due to export size limitations or platform restrictions. This highlighted an important lesson that exported datasets may not always fully represent the underlying system data, and analysts must be aware of these limitations when interpreting results.

Working with the JSON files themselves was smoother than expected. Because the structure was predictable, most of the effort shifted toward feature engineering and deciding how to analyze behavior meaningfully. Creating datetime variables such as year, weekday, and hour allowed me to explore patterns that were not immediately visible in the raw data. This reinforced the idea that much of data analysis involves preparing data before visualization or interpretation can begin.

I really enjoyed this assignment because it felt different from something like YouTube Wrapped generated automatically by Google. Instead of receiving pre-made statistics, I was able to perform a hands-on analysis and explore my own data directly. It was especially interesting to look at the heatmap visualizations and recognize patterns in my habits, then reflect on why those patterns might exist. Doing the analysis manually made the results feel more meaningful and engaging.

One frustration I encountered was during the content category analysis. A large portion of the videos ended up classified as "Other" or "Uncategorized," which accounted for more than half of the dataset. This showed me that automated categorization is more complex than I initially expected. If I had more time, I would focus on improving the classification logic or experimenting with more advanced text-processing techniques to reduce the number of uncategorized videos and produce more accurate thematic groupings.

AI tools played a supporting role throughout the project. I mainly used AI as a coding assistant to help understand JSON structures, troubleshoot errors, and explore possible visualization ideas. While AI was helpful for suggesting approaches, it often required adjustments and verification before the solutions worked correctly. In several cases, suggested code needed modification to match my dataset structure, which forced me to understand the logic rather than simply copying solutions. This showed me that AI can speed up learning but does not replace problem-solving or critical thinking.

Overall, this project helped me better understand how messy real-world data can be and how important iteration and experimentation are in analytics work. I also gained a clearer understanding of how AI tools can be integrated responsibly into a workflow as assistants rather than replacements for learning.
